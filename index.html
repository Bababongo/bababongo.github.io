The translation layer

The translation layer is where AI protein design becomes real. A model that generates sequences is not the same as a biological objective made testable.

AI protein design does not succeed only because a model generates sequences. It succeeds when a biological objective becomes a well-defined design problem, when the right residues are fixed or redesigned, when input structures and ligand / interface poses are questioned, when generated variants are filtered with biochemical judgment, and when wet-lab data teaches the next round. My work focuses on that translation layer — the space between design hypotheses and experimentally validated protein function.

02 / Method stack

How I turn design hypotheses into experiments

The workflow layer I am building across enzymes, antibodies, and protein interfaces — from biological objective to validated biomolecule and back.

This is the operating system I am building: not model worship, not brute-force screening, but a disciplined loop for turning design hypotheses into data.

01

Define the biological objective

Activity, affinity, specificity, substrate scope, developability, expression, product formation.

02

Translate biology into constraints

Fixed versus designable regions, ligand / interface assumptions, catalytic or binding-site preservation, and assay-aware success criteria.

03

Choose or adapt the model workflow

ProteinMPNN-style, LigandMPNN-style, PPI-MPNN-style, AF3 / structure prediction, and BindCraft-style binder filters.

04

Generate under multiple assumptions

Alternative structural inputs, alternative pose assumptions, different masks, and controlled sampling settings.

05

Rank variants

Model score, structural sanity, catalytic / interface geometry, homologue plausibility, expression risk, diversity, testability.

06

Build and validate

gBlocks, cloning, expression, FACS, ELISA, BLI, GC-MS, LC-MS, biochemical assays.

07

Learn and redesign

Failed variants update constraints; successful variants seed next rounds; active learning closes the loop.

05 / About

About Eze

I'm Ezechinyere "Eze" Ukabiala, a UC Berkeley / UCSF Bioengineering PhD candidate in the Keasling Lab. My work sits at the interface of protein engineering, enzyme redesign, antibody / interface validation, metabolic engineering, analytical chemistry, and AI-assisted design workflows.

I am especially interested in the translation layer where computational protein-design hypotheses become real experiments: which residues to fix, which regions to redesign, which variants to order, which assays to trust, and what failure teaches the next round.

I am not trying to replace experiments with models. I am interested in making models useful enough that the next experiment is smarter. My current scientific direction connects microbial production, enzyme redesign, AI-assisted protein-design workflows, and analytical validation. Details of active thesis and protein-ML projects are intentionally limited while the work is ongoing.
