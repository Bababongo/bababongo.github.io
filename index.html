The missing layer

The missing layer

Most AI-biology stories stop at the model or the result. The hard part lives between them.

A protein design model can generate sequences, rank mutations, or propose structures. But a biological objective still has to be made testable. A variant still has to be buildable. An assay still has to measure the thing that matters. A readout still has to be interpreted without confusing noise, proxy, and function.

That middle layer is the campaign.

The campaign is where prediction becomes evidence.

The translation layer

A model that generates sequences is not the same as a biological objective made testable.

I work in the translation layer between model behavior and experimental reality. The work is not only choosing sequences. It is deciding what question the model is allowed to answer, what constraints biology imposes, what variants can actually be built, what assay can adjudicate the claim, and what the next round should learn.

01 / Campaign logic

The campaign is the unit of work

A protein design campaign is not a model output. It is a learning system. The model proposes structure in the search space. The scientist decides which hypotheses are worth spending biology on. The assay turns a claim into evidence. The next round changes because the last one taught you something.

Objective → Constraint → Prior → Panel → Readout → Update. Every round tightens the question the model is allowed to answer.

02 / Principles

Principles I work from

Field notes for operating at the boundary where computational priors become experimental decisions.

01

The model is a prior, not a verdict.

A high score is a reason to run the assay. It is not the result.

02

The campaign is the product.

A design only matters if the loop can build it, measure it, learn from it, and improve it.

03

The assay is part of the design.

A weak readout can make a good model look bad and a bad model look good.

04

A null result is information only if the experiment was diagnostic.

Failure has to localize the broken layer: expression, folding, binding, chemistry, assay, or detection.

05

Different proteins, same campaign grammar.

Enzymes transform. Antibodies recognize. But both require objectives, constraints, measurements, and tradeoffs.

06

The human defines the search space.

AI expands what can be searched. Scientific judgment decides what should be searched.

06 / Research

Research under experimental constraint

My PhD work sits in enzyme engineering and biomanufacturing. The broader question is general: how do you make a biological objective survive the trip through model, variant, assay, and analytical readout?

Mechanism-aware enzyme engineering

What has to be true chemically for this design to work?

I treat mechanism as the design specification: substrate geometry, cofactor behavior, reaction context, and measured product formation decide whether a model hypothesis survived contact with chemistry.

cofactorssubstrate geometrynon-native chemistryanalytical readouts

Model-guided variant design

Which model outputs are worth spending biology on?

I decide what biological question the model is allowed to answer, then translate scores, structures, and constraints into ranked panels that can be built and interpreted.

sequencestructureconstraintsranked panelsbuildability

Assay-driven learning

Did the biology fail, or did the measurement fail to see it?

I use controls, sensitivity, and diagnostic readouts to separate true biological failure from expression, folding, assay, detection, or interpretation failure.

controlssensitivityGC/MSLC/MSdiagnostic readouts

Antibody and binder campaign logic

Which readout decides the campaign: discovery, kinetics, or developability?

Discovery, affinity maturation, and developability are different model-to-measurement problems. Each needs a different panel, assay, and decision rule.

10 / Boundaries

Boundaries

I am not presenting myself as a foundation-model architect. My edge is operating at the model-to-measurement boundary: framing biological objectives, running and adapting protein-design workflows, building testable panels, interpreting assays, and turning measured outcomes into the next round.

The point is not to replace ML specialists or experimental specialists. The point is to connect them without losing the biology at the handoff.
