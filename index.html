The translation layer

A model that generates sequences is not the same as a biological objective made testable. The campaign is the unit of work.

I work in the translation layer between model behavior and experimental reality. The work is not only choosing sequences. It is defining the objective, framing what the model can usefully optimize, deciding what can be built, choosing the assay, reading the instrument, and using the result to design the next round.

01 / Campaign logic

Campaign logic

A protein design campaign is not a model output. It is a loop. The model proposes structure in the search space, but the assay decides what the biology actually did.

Objective in, next design out. Every round tightens the question the model is allowed to answer.

01

Objective

What the biology needs.

02

Model prior

What computation can rank.

03

Variant set

What can be built and tested.

04

Assay

What measures the objective.

05

Readout

What the instrument actually says.

06

Next design

What the data changes.

The readout reframes the objective, and the loop repeats. That closed cycle, not any single model, is the campaign.

04 / Research

Model-guided protein engineering under real experimental constraints

My PhD work is in enzyme engineering and biomanufacturing; my industry work is in therapeutic antibodies. Transformation-side design depth and recognition-side campaign experience inform each other: holding both changes how I read either one. The through-line is a single question: how do you turn a biological objective into a model-guided campaign that survives contact with measurement?

Enzyme engineering and non-native chemistry

Transformation-side depth: mechanism-aware protein engineering where function is defined by chemistry, substrate geometry, cofactor behavior, and measured product formation.

mechanismcofactorssubstrate scopeanalytical readouts
Grounding: thesis work on enzymes for non-native chemistry, with design hypotheses confirmed by GC/MS and LC/MS product detection.

Cross-modality range: antibodies and enzymes

Recognition-side campaign experience on therapeutic antibodies, carried alongside transformation-side enzyme depth. Holding both means reading discovery, affinity maturation, and developability as distinct model-to-bench problems, each with its own decisive readout.

FACSELISAEC50 by FACSBLI (Octet, Gator)
Grounding: industry therapeutic-antibody discovery: FACS and ELISA screening, EC50 by FACS, and BLI binding kinetics on Octet and Gator. Carrying enzyme depth alongside widens the range of problems I can read, not narrows it.

Assay-driven learning

Using functional assays and analytical chemistry to separate true design failures from assay limits, sensitivity floors, and interpretation errors: the difference between a model that is wrong and a measurement that could not see.

GC/MSLC/MScontrolsDBTL
Grounding: built low-micromolar GC/MS detection for fatty acids and terminal alkenes; treats analytical validation as design infrastructure.

Model-guided variant design

Turning model outputs into buildable variant sets, then using experimental data to decide which hypotheses survive: training and fine-tuning sequence-to-function models, ranking under uncertainty, and choosing what to build.

inverse foldingstructure-conditionedfine-tuningcalibration
Grounding: inverse-folding and structure-conditioned design workflows on HPC, evaluated by sequence recovery, likelihood, and calibration against held-out data.

The public site reveals how I think, not what I found. Unpublished substrates, mutation sets, residues, titers, and confidential results are intentionally withheld while the work is active.

P08.3 / 10Completed portfolio artifact

Mechanism-sliced zero-shot protein fitness

A model-family benchmark for where protein models work inside enzymes.

Built a reproducible Python benchmark comparing ESM-2, MSA conservation, and ProteinMPNN on enzyme DMS assays. The key result: global mutation ranking and mechanism-local behavior can diverge. On VIM-2, ProteinMPNN is strongest overall, but much weaker at curated metal-binding residues, showing that whole-protein performance can hide mechanism-local failure modes.

P17.4 / 10Strong prototype

Data-efficiency curves for protein fitness learning

How many experimental labels does it take to beat zero-shot?

I built a benchmark that starts from P0 zero-shot ESM scores, then asks how many measured mutation labels are needed before supervised correction improves performance. Around 384 labels, residual correction begins to beat zero-shot on real enzyme datasets, but label value depends on selection strategy, mechanism region, and landscape coverage.
