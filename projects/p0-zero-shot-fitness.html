Summary

The result in one minute

P0 evaluates whether a protein language model predicts experimental mutation effects equally well across different biological regions of an enzyme. Using public TEM-1 beta-lactamase DMS data, I scored 4,783 single amino-acid substitutions with ESM-2 and compared model scores to experimental fitness.

The result was nuanced. ESM-2 captured meaningful zero-shot signal, and scaling from 8M to 35M parameters improved performance. But the biologically important result was not only the overall score. Exact UniProt active-site residues formed a small, uncertain, lower-performing slice, while broader ligand-contact and active-site-neighborhood regions showed stronger model signal.

The lesson: model evaluation should not stop at aggregate performance. It should ask where the model works, where it fails, and whether those failure modes line up with the biology that matters.

01 / The question

The question

Do protein language models fail differently on catalytic and mechanism-relevant residues than on the rest of the protein?

This question matters because protein engineering rarely cares about average residues. It cares about the parts of a protein that determine whether a molecule binds, folds, reacts, expresses, or can be measured. A model can rank mutations well overall and still be weakest where chemistry matters most.

03 / The annotation logic

The annotation logic

UniProt active site

57 variants

Positions 68, 71, 128, 164. Exact catalytic core.

UniProt substrate-binding site

55 variants

Positions 232 to 234. Pocket residues adjacent to the catalytic core.

PDB 1M40 ligand contact, 5 A

277 variants

Residues in chain A with any heavy atom within 5.0 A of the CB4 inhibitor.

Active-site neighborhood

461 variants

Broader mechanism-adjacent region around validated active-site and substrate-binding positions.

The exact active-site group is intentionally narrow and biologically meaningful, but statistically small. The broader groups test whether the model captures constraints around the functional environment, not only the catalytic core.

04 / The scoring method

The scoring method

score = log P(mutant aa | masked wild-type context) - log P(wild-type aa | masked wild-type context)
mutant aa: proposed substitution wild-type aa: original residue masked position: model sees context only

For each mutation, I masked the wild-type position and asked ESM-2 how plausible the mutant amino acid was relative to the original residue in the same sequence context. The model was not trained on this DMS assay. This is a zero-shot variant-effect prediction test.

05 / The results

The results

Overall Spearman improved from 0.4113 with ESM-2 8M to 0.5548 with ESM-2 35M. Exact UniProt active-site performance also improved, from 0.3023 to 0.4596, but remained below the non-active-site background. Broader ligand-contact and active-site-neighborhood slices showed stronger signal, reaching 0.7127 and 0.7027 with ESM-2 35M.

06 / Interpretation

What the result means

The strongest result is not a single number. It is the shape of the evaluation. ESM-2 captures meaningful constraints in TEM-1, and scaling helps. But aggregate performance hides the biological structure of the model's behavior. Exact catalytic residues are a small and uncertain slice. Broader active-site environments show strong signal. Mutation classes behave differently. The benchmark therefore moves the question from "does the model work?" to "where does the model earn trust?"

A model's average performance is not enough. The engineering question is where the model deserves experimental trust.

09 / The boundary of the claim

What the artifact earns, and where it stops

Limitations

What this does not claim

  • one enzyme
  • one assay context
  • one sequence-only model family
  • active-site-only group is small
  • ligand-contact group comes from one inhibitor-bound structure
  • DMS fitness is not pure catalytic chemistry
  • no structure-aware baseline yet
  • no prospective design campaign yet

These limitations do not weaken the project. They make the claim precise.

11 / The takeaway

The takeaway

P0 is a benchmark for asking whether protein language model performance changes near catalytic and mechanism-relevant regions of an enzyme.

The main lesson is simple: overall model performance is not enough. In AI biology, the interesting question is where the model works, where it fails, and whether those failure modes line up with the biology a scientist actually cares about.

The model score is the beginning of the question, not the end of the experiment.

P0 is one enzyme, one assay context, and one sequence-only model family. It is an evaluation layer, built with biological judgment, not a finished design platform.
