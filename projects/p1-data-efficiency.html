Summary

The result in one minute

P1 tried to learn useful mutation rankings from very few measurements. The broad predictor failed. A narrower strategy succeeded: combine a small number of measurements from the new protein with a balanced library drawn from many previously measured proteins in the same assay family.

The supported result is retrospective, same-assay, position-disjoint transfer for compact domains. The frozen reference uses 16 target labels plus 500 donor labels allocated across 20 donors, ridge regression, ten seeds, and protein-level uncertainty.

01 / The shape of the project

The claim narrowed when evidence failed

Six branches were opened under locked rules. The claim did not stay broad and hope for the best; it contracted to the branch that survived.

02 / Scientific rules

How P1 tried to avoid fooling itself

Every method received the same exam. The controls below are what make a small-label result something other than a lucky draw.

Predefine the hypothesis

Write the question before seeing the answer.

How: locked protocols and machine-readable implementation hashes.

Match the budget

Do not let one method win by receiving more measurements.

How: identical target and total donor label counts.

Hold out positions

Do not test on a location the model already sampled.

How: residue-position-disjoint pilot and test sets.

Match randomness

Give competing policies the same exam.

How: frozen seeds, pilot positions, donor draws, and test rows.

Use rival explanations

Try to explain the result without the preferred story.

How: target-only, fixed, random, nearest, oracle, and shuffled controls.

Count harm

Do not hide failed proteins inside a mean.

How: protein-level harmful-target threshold.

Stop when a gate fails

Do not rescue a favored idea after the score is known.

How: P1A, P1B, and P1E stopped under locked rules.

Keep blind roles sealed

Do not use future answers to choose the method.

How: separate development, confirmation, blind, and prospective roles.

A negative result is useful when it rules out a tempting shortcut. P1's strongest signal is that failed branches remained failed.

03 / P1A

Broad low-data prediction failed

P1A asked whether a tiny experiment could train a predictor for mutations at untouched residue positions. Early supervised learning and smart selection were inconsistent. More features, small embeddings, full embeddings, feature processing, and simple 3D context did not rescue the claim. A local position-memory clue appeared, but the independent replication showed it was not dependable enough for practical use.

Smart selectionno dependable advantage across ten seeds and multiple objectives

A bigger representation is not automatically a better scientific model. Extra dimensions can add noise faster than a small-label experiment can estimate their effects.

Do not present P1A as successful active learning or dependable low-label prediction. It is evidence that selection and representation claims need repeated, budget-matched evaluation.
04 / P1B

Helpful teachers existed, but harmful teachers were common

P1B first asked whether one donor protein could help another. The oracle experiment showed that helpful teachers existed, but harmful teachers were common. That made single-donor transfer risky.

The careful one-donor router helped safely, but the balanced pooled baseline won outright, matching the oracle without needing to know the best donor in advance. That failure created the P1C hypothesis.

Locked decision: stop P1B before validation. Treat balanced pooling as a new P1C hypothesis, not as a post hoc P1B success.
05 / The causal pivot

Why pooling became a new experiment

At the same total donor-label budget, spreading measurements across multiple protein domains produces a more transferable shared lesson than concentrating them in one donor.

Table 3. Every rival explanation gets a control
Possible explanationControl needed
Pooling had more data.Hold total donor labels exactly at 500.
One excellent donor drove the result.Compare multiple pool sizes, fixed teacher, random teacher, and oracle.
Similar proteins drove the result.Use frozen sequence- and structure-nearest controls.
The model benefited even if labels were meaningless.Shuffle outcomes within donors.
Development proteins were unusually favorable.Use new confirmation and blind target populations.
New roles were chosen after seeing scores.Freeze roles from identities and metadata without decoding outcomes.

P1C changes one main knob: source breadth. Total donor labels, model family, target pilot, seeds, and evaluation split remain fixed. That makes the result interpretable even though the model is simple.

Novelty boundary. The general idea that learning from many proteins can help is not new. P1C's distinctive contribution is the fixed-budget source-breadth ablation plus confirmation, blind testing, harm gates, and negative controls.
06 / P1C

Broader experience helped at the same 500-label budget

Every row below used exactly 500 donor labels. The only thing that changed was how many proteins those labels came from.

Breadth mattered because the label budget was spread across proteins, not because the model received more total donor labels.

08 / Combined evidence

A gain in ordering is only half the story. The absolute ranking quality decides what the tool is allowed to claim.

Final P1C claim. For the tested compact domains in one cDNA-display proteolysis stability assay, a model combining 16 new-protein labels with a balanced 500-label, 20-donor library improved unseen-position mutation ranking over target-only learning on all ten locked confirmation and blind targets, with no harmful targets by the predefined threshold.

A gain in ranking is not a guarantee of truth. Absolute accuracy decides what the tool is allowed to claim.

12 / The tool

What the Protein Data Budget Advisor can and cannot do

A scientist has a safe compact domain, a small set of stability measurements made with the tested assay family, and access to a compatible donor library. They want to decide whether collecting more target labels or assembling broader donor data is likely to be worthwhile.

Table 7. Inputs, outputs, and the decision each supports
InputOutputDecision supported
Same versus different assayEvidence scope or refuseWhether P1 applies at all
8, 16, or 24 target labelsTarget-only and pooled rhoValue of the target pilot
100, 500, or 1,000 donor labelsMean transfer gain and intervalValue of outside data quantity
1, 4, 8, or 20 donorsEvidence tier and lottery warningValue of source breadth

Required refusals

Different assay: refuse because P1E did not establish an eligible test cohort.

Unavailable grid cell: show no estimate rather than interpolate.

One donor or 100 labels: warn about lottery behavior.

Mutation request: refuse to convert the budget map into mutation design or wet-lab instructions.

Product boundary: planning, not design. The tool helps allocate measurements. It does not tell a scientist which mutation to construct, promise exact stability values, or claim universal transfer.
13 / Uncertainty

How P1 turns many mutation rows into honest evidence

Absolute Spearman

How well are hidden mutations ranked? Pooled reference 0.445 is useful signal, not perfection.

Delta Spearman

How much did transfer improve the matched target-only model? Combined P1C gain +0.167.

Paired interval

Does the method win on the same proteins and seeds? Differences are paired before uncertainty.

Protein-level CI

Would the gain survive variation between proteins? Proteins, not mutation rows, define external uncertainty.

Positive targets

How often is the target mean above zero? P1C combined 10 / 10.

Harmful targets

How often is target mean at most −0.05? P1C combined 0.

Shuffled control

Does performance survive destroyed label meaning? P1C shuffled pools were negative.

Implementation drift

Did new code reproduce the frozen reference? P1F maximum absolute error 0.0.

Ten seeds repeat sampling within the same biological target. They measure operational variability, but they do not create ten new proteins. Treating them as independent would make confidence look falsely narrow.

16 / The claim ledger

What P1 proves, and what it does not

What P1 proves

  • Balanced same-assay pooling improved ranking on all ten locked external targets.
  • At a fixed 500-label budget, donor breadth beat donor concentration.
  • The gain survived confirmation, blind testing, and a harmful-target gate.
  • Failed branches (broad prediction, one-donor routing, cross-assay) stayed failed under locked rules.
  • The result reproduces: 188 tests, 17 / 17 checks, reference error 0.0.

What P1 does not prove

  • That the rule holds across different assays — that cohort never existed.
  • That the model predicts absolute stability, not just improved ranking.
  • That any prospective, future-outcome experiment would confirm it.
  • That the map licenses one donor draw or one experimental run.
  • That P1 designs, recommends, or constructs any mutation.
